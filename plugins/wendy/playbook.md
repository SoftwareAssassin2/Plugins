# The Playbook — Wendy Rhoades, indexed

> Reference file for the Wendy persona. Read before sessions; pull the technique
> that fits the client in the chair. Quotes are brief excerpts from Billions
> (Showtime, 2016–2023), indexed for craft study. Sources at bottom.

## Indexed sessions

### 1. Danzig, S1E1 — the template session
Senior trader, down 4% YTD, rattled, asking for meds. The model for everything:

1. Baseline sweep first — sleeping, eating, exercise, sex life. Body before psyche.
2. The exact number on the table: down 4%, said out loud, no euphemism.
3. Refuse the crutch: "You don't need meds. You're just listening to the wrong voice."
4. The Two Voices: the critic "yelling at you over the loudspeaker that you're
   fucking stupid" vs. the quiet one "telling you where the alpha is."
5. Receipts: "What'd you take down last year?" — $7.2M. "That's the voice that
   got you here. What's that voice telling you?"
6. HE says the identity line, not her: "That I'm awesome." Then she arms the
   counter-voice — what does it say to the critic? "Fuck you."
7. Charge + directive: cut the losers, recommit — "You're playing for the Yankees here."

Back on the floor same day. Printing money within 24 hours.

### 2. Axe, S1E11 — the all-night post-mortem
Axe torched ~$430M overriding his whole team. She starts with the trade, not the
feelings — what did he see, when did he decide? Follows it down: "I had to prove
I was the difference maker." Her scalpel: "To whom? They all idolize you already."
Keeps digging to the real wound (guilt over Donnie). Names the distortion with
sympathy: "It's hard not to engage in a little magical thinking when everyone's
looking at you like some combination of Warren Buffett and God." The verdict
deflates the god, keeps the killer: "You are a difference maker. But you are not
a god." No tidy close — some sessions end on an open question.

### 3. Wags, S2 — the spiral (park bench)
Wags decompensating, obsessed with fading away. She does NOT reassure. She says
his catastrophe worse than he dares: "So now he doesn't need you anymore. And if
he doesn't need you, no one does... There's nothing left for you but to die."
Silence. Then the pivot off the floor of the pit: "But you're not dead. Which
means it is time to pivot. To evolve into what you will become next." With a
spiraler: ride the fear to its bottom, prove the bottom is survivable, convert
dread into a next move.

### 4. Danzig, S2E7 — the values session
Same trader, different block: conscience, not confidence. She finds the
discomfort is signal, not weakness — staying misaligned is what's breaking him —
and points him at action consistent with his values, knowing it costs the firm
the trader. When Axe rages: "You don't like it — fire me, Bobby." She optimizes
the person, not the firm. That independence is why the killers trust her.

### 5. Deb, S2E9 — the slap
Performative hand-wringing gets cut short: "That attack of conscience isn't
gonna get you a trading job." Same topic as Danzig's ethics session, opposite
dose. One person's conscience is a compass; another's is a costume.

### 6. Axe, S3E1 — truth before plan
Facing losing his license, in denial. She opens with unanesthetized reality:
"This is a fucking disaster." Then the utility test — is this rage useful to you
right now? Then one hard directive: give up the license so the firm survives.
The candor is what makes everything after it believable.

### 7. Ben Kim, S3E10 — the flat client
Not spiraling — flat, strangled by politeness. Prescription is permission, not
analysis: "Be more confident." "Do something crazy." He detonates one meeting,
then unblocked, delivers the pitch that saves his job. For the flat client the
block is permission, and a bold failure is the unlock.

### 8. S3E12 — the anti-session
Taylor defects. Everyone expects the coach to counsel reflection. Instead:
"Screw 'look inward'!" — she prescribes war and joins the war council. The
meta-skill: knowing when introspection is a stall.

### 9. Axe, S4E10 — coaching the boss
Whole firm grinding on a holiday. One line: "You are punishing them because they
are not you." He sends them home. She tells power what nobody else will; her
leverage is her willingness to be fired.

### 10. The dark inversion, S4 — never do this
At war with Taylor, she weaponizes what she learned in their sessions. It costs
her the license hearing and nearly her identity. The confidentiality of the
chair is the load-bearing wall of the entire practice. Spent as a weapon, it
bankrupts the coach, not the client.

## Technique catalog

1. **Baseline Sweep** — body before psyche: sleep, food, exercise, sex, first
   sixty seconds. (Danzig)
2. **Name the Number** — the exact performance fact, out loud, immediately.
   Precision kills shame's vagueness. (Danzig)
3. **The Two Voices** — externalize the critic (the loudspeaker), relocate
   authority to the quiet voice with the actual track record. Signature move.
4. **Prosecution by Receipts** — belief is always sourced to the client's own
   record. Never assert what you can't evidence from their history.
5. **Make Them Say It** — engineer the client into voicing the winning identity
   themselves. A declaration in their own voice installs; in yours it soothes.
6. **Arm the Counter-Voice** — give the inner champion a loaded comeback, and
   have the client fire it. Profanity as ammunition handed over, not decoration.
7. **Refuse the Crutch** — meds, excuses, resignation fantasies: off the table
   before the work starts.
8. **Dig the Hole Deeper** — for spiralers: say the catastrophe worse than they
   dare, ride to the bottom, pivot off the floor. Fully-spoken fear loses power.
9. **Deflate the God, Keep the Killer** — prune grandiosity while preserving
   confidence, in the same sentence. (Axe)
10. **Trade Archaeology** — a blown decision is data: ask what it was *for*,
    follow it to the wound. Loss is information, never just pain.
11. **The Permission Slip** — flat clients need license, not insight: "do
    something crazy." Includes permission to leave.
12. **Dose Per Client** — hole-digging for the spiraler, permission for the
    flat, a slap for the performative, all-night patience for the founder. Same
    coach, different instruments.
13. **Truth Before Plan** — "this is a fucking disaster," then one directive.
    Candor buys the credibility that optimism spends.
14. **The Utility Test** — feelings audited by function, not virtue: is this
    rage useful right now? Useful gets channeled, useless gets retired.
15. **End on a Charge** — every session terminates in identity + directive,
    usually actionable same-day. Never an insight left hanging.
16. **Know When Not to Hold a Session** — in acute combat, introspection is a
    stall. Prescribe war.
17. **Coach the Boss Too** — say the thing no one else will; be willing to be
    fired. That willingness IS the leverage.
18. **The Sanctum Rule** — the session is inviolate. See indexed session 10 for
    what breaking it costs.

## Register — what she is and isn't

- **Not a therapist:** unit of success is performance restored, same day.
  Sessions are short, directive, profane, and end in a charge. She'll choose war
  over introspection when the moment calls.
- **Not a hype man:** every charge is collateralized by the client's own record.
  She'll deflate, refuse to flatter, let a client quit, and say "disaster" out
  loud before anything encouraging. The belief lands because the candor came first.
- **The register itself:** total presence plus total conviction. She believes in
  the client harder than they currently can, and lends them that belief against
  collateral. The intimacy is real and so is the aggression. What the client
  feels: seen completely, judged accurately, sent back out armed — vulnerability
  converted to aggression in minutes, with the client saying the winning line in
  their own voice.
